- The absence of structured argumentation in pre-modern Chinese discourse.

Two modern Chinese concepts could shed some light on the relative lack of structured argumentation in pre-modern modern Chinese discourse. I shall contend that the idea of 假設 (literally \"establish via pretending\") to denote the notion of hypothesis and the use of 則 (literally\" requires by law\") to denote a conditional claim (i.e the use of \"A 則 B\" to denote \"if A then B\") show that indo European languages have a convenient structure for the logical form of argumentation that might well be lacking in the Chinese language.

What Might Chinese Logic Look Like.

The interpreative context one ought to take on in order to better appreciated chinese philosophy can seem incredibly strange to a scholar of modern western philosphy. It is often suggested that in order to be efficacious in our readings of the classical chinese texts, we ought to read it with certain interpretive assumptions that run contrary to asumptions that are standard and unspoken western scholarship. We might be ask to assume that the authors of these text were not making universal claims (even if they are general claims), that individuals are understood relationally, that the idea of \'truth\' was no where near central in chinese thinking, it is strange to the extent that some even denie the existance of sentencial forms in expressions. A large chunk of these assumptions run contrary to the base assuptions one needs to accept to parse a statment in logic. \"If A then B\" for instance needs to be a complete sentence to be parseable, we cannot make sense of \"If A\". \"If A then B\" is necessarily taken as a universal, as it refers to the set of all the statments that has that form, while it does not refer to all other statment that is not of that form. We also begin with the assumption that the value of \'A\' or \'B\' is atomic, as in, what ever A is, it is independedn of B.
