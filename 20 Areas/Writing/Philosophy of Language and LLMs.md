## Theories of Meaning in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy offers a rich discussion of theories of meaning, distinguishing between **semantic theories**, which aim to assign meanings to expressions, and **metasemantic theories**, which explore the underlying reasons why expressions have the meanings they do [1]. This distinction echoes our previous conversation about **Peirce's sign theory**, where we differentiated between the **interpretant** (the understanding of a sign) and the factors that determine a sign's meaning (like conventions or practical effects).

### Semantic Theories: A Variety of Approaches

The sources outline several prominent approaches to semantic theorising, many of which fall under the umbrella of **classical semantic theories** [2]. These theories generally agree that sentences can be true or false, and that their truth values depend on the information they convey, often referred to as the **proposition** expressed by the sentence.

- **The Theory of Reference** forms the foundation for many semantic theories, connecting expressions with their contributions to a sentence's truth value [3]. It focuses on the referent, the object or concept an expression picks out in the world. For instance, the name "London" refers to the city of London, and this reference helps determine the truth of sentences containing that name.
- **Possible Worlds Semantics** takes a broader perspective, representing the meaning of a sentence as a function from possible worlds to truth values [4]. Each possible world represents a complete way the world could be. The **intension** of a sentence, its meaning, determines its truth value in each possible world. This allows for analysing the truth of sentences across different hypothetical scenarios.
- **Russellian Semantics** views propositions as complexes composed of objects, properties, and relations [5]. For instance, the sentence "London is in England" expresses a proposition involving the objects London and England and the relation of "being in." This proposition is true in the actual world if the relation holds between those specific objects.
- **Fregean Semantics** introduces the concept of **sense**, a mode of presentation of an object, adding a cognitive dimension to meaning [6]. While "The Morning Star" and "The Evening Star" both refer to Venus, they express different senses, reflecting different ways of thinking about the same object. This distinction allows Fregean semantics to address cases where expressions with the same reference seem to differ in meaning.

The sources also present **alternatives to classical semantic theories**:

- **Davidsonian Semantics** eschews the notion of "meanings as entities" and instead uses Tarski-style truth theories to characterise meaning [7]. This approach defines truth for a language and derives meanings from these truth conditions.
- **Internalist Semantics** ties meaning to mental representations [8]. It suggests that the meaning of an expression is an instruction for building or accessing specific mental representations within a language user's mind.
- **Inferentialist Semantics** emphasizes the inferential relations between sentences in determining meaning [9]. The meaning of a sentence is determined by its role in inferences, highlighting the interconnectedness of language.
- **Dynamic Semantics** focuses on the dynamic nature of meaning construction through language interaction [10]. It highlights how language use can alter the context of conversation and shape the interpretation of subsequent utterances.
- **Expressivist Semantics** proposes that certain expressions, particularly in moral and aesthetic language, function primarily to express attitudes or emotions rather than describing the world [10].

### Metasemantic Theories: Exploring the Foundations of Meaning

Metasemantic theories aim to go beyond simply assigning meanings and explore the deeper question of what determines meaning. They seek to identify the facts in virtue of which expressions acquire their specific semantic properties [10]. This parallels questions in other areas of philosophy, such as the nature of intentional action or personal identity.

The sources discuss various approaches to metasemantics:

- **Meaning Skepticism** denies the possibility of a definitive and informative explanation of meaning [11]. This view treats meaning as a primitive concept, not reducible to other facts.
- **Meaning, Belief, and Convention** emphasizes the role of conventions established within a community of language users in grounding meaning [11]. Conventions connect expressions with specific objects or concepts, enabling shared understanding and communication.
- **The Gricean Program**, drawing on the work of philosopher H.P. Grice, focuses on the speaker's intentions and the hearer's recognition of those intentions in communication [12]. Meaning emerges from the speaker's intention to convey an idea and the hearer's successful inference of that intention. This highlights the role of mental states and reasoning in communication.
- **Mental Representation-Based Theories** directly link linguistic meaning to mental representations [12]. These theories propose that language processing involves pairing linguistic expressions with mental representations, and the meaning of an expression is inherited from the content of the associated mental representation.
- **Causal Origin** points to the causal history of an expression as a factor shaping its meaning [13]. The specific causal chain leading to the use of an expression contributes to its interpretation.
- **Truth-Maximization and the Principle of Charity**, often associated with philosopher Donald Davidson, suggest that we interpret others' utterances by assuming they are largely truthful and rational [13]. This principle guides our understanding of language by promoting coherence and minimizing inconsistencies.
- **Reference Magnetism**, primarily discussed in the context of proper names, proposes that reference has a powerful influence on meaning [13]. The referent of a name exerts a "magnetic pull" on its semantic content, suggesting a close connection between reference and meaning.
- **Rules of Use** highlight the rules governing the use of expressions in a language as determinants of meaning [13]. These rules, acquired through social interaction and language learning, constrain how expressions are appropriately used in different contexts.

It's important to note that these are just some of the main approaches to meaning explored in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy. The field of philosophy of language is vast and constantly evolving, offering numerous perspectives and ongoing debates about the nature of meaning.

## Theories of Meaning and Normativity

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy outlines a variety of approaches to the theory of meaning, broadly categorised as semantic theories and metasemantic theories [1].

- **Semantic theories** aim to define the meanings of words and sentences in a language [1].
- **Metasemantic theories**, sometimes referred to as "foundational theories of meaning" or a "metaphysics of meaning", seek to explain the facts that give those expressions their meanings [1].

### Semantic Theories

- **Classical semantic theories** centre around the notion of truth conditions and the proposition expressed by a sentence [2]. They explain how the meanings of words, in conjunction with the context, determine the proposition and, consequently, the truth conditions of a sentence [2].

Several prominent versions of classical semantic theories exist:

- **Possible worlds semantics** uses the concept of possible worlds to define truth. A sentence is considered true if it holds in all possible worlds where the relevant conditions are met [3].
- **Russellian semantics** focuses on the objects, properties, and relations that expressions refer to. Sentences are true if the objects, properties, and relations they pick out stand in the correct relationship to each other [4].
- **Fregean semantics** introduces the concept of "sense," which goes beyond mere reference and captures the way an object is presented [4]. This approach aims to resolve Frege’s puzzle, where sentences with the same truth value seem to differ in meaning [5].

Several alternatives to classical semantic theories challenge these assumptions:

- **Davidsonian semantics** argues against treating meanings as entities and instead favours a truth-conditional approach based on Tarski's work [6]. The focus here is on formulating truth theories that capture the truth conditions of sentences without recourse to intensions or other abstract entities [6].
- **Internalist semantics** posits that meaning is determined by mental representations within the language user's mind [7]. This approach rejects the idea that meaning is determined by external factors like reference [7].
- **Inferentialist semantics** emphasises the role of inferential relations in determining meaning [7]. It suggests that a sentence's meaning is constituted by its place within a web of inferences [7].
- **Dynamic semantics** views meaning as an evolving process, shaped by the flow of conversation and the context of utterance [8].
- **Expressivist semantics** focuses on the function of language in expressing mental states, such as beliefs or desires [8].

### Metasemantic Theories

- **Metasemantic theories**, in contrast to semantic theories, seek to answer the question: _What makes it the case that expressions have the meanings they do?_ [9]. They aim to explain the grounding of semantic facts [9].

Many philosophers working within classical semantics try to explain the truth conditions of sentences [10]. However, the approach to metasemantics may shift if one adopts a different conception of semantic facts, like the internalist approach [10].

### Normative Theories of Meaning

**Normative theories of meaning** tie the concept of meaning to notions of rationality, justification, and correctness [11]. They argue that understanding meaning involves understanding **norms of language use** [11].

The discussion of normative theories often revolves around the relationship between "means" and "ought."

- **The weak thesis of the normativity of meaning (Kripke-Wittgenstein thesis)** asserts that "means implies ought" [12]. Knowing the meaning of a term implies knowing how to correctly use it [12].
- **The strong thesis of the normativity of meaning** argues that meaning can be fully defined in normative terms [12]. This implies a reciprocal relationship between "means" and "ought": Every "means" implies an "ought", and every "ought" determines a "means" [12].

These theses raise questions about the nature of meaning, the role of community in determining meaning, and the potential conflict between solipsistic and communitarian perspectives on meaning [13, 14].

**Gibbard [11, 15] suggests** that the question of whether meaning is normative is **a meta-theoretical one**, concerning the meaning of 'meaning' itself. He argues that **normative and solipsistic or communitarian views are not mutually exclusive**. This is because the dispute between solipsistic and communitarian views pertains to the **substantive nature of meaning** - the facts that determine meaning - rather than the **meta-theoretical question of whether meaning is normative**.

The normativity of meaning is also explored in the context of **truth**, specifically **deflationary theories of truth** [16]. Deflationary theories suggest that the concept of truth is fully captured by the schema _"p" is true if and only if p_ [16]. This approach denies that truth is a substantive property requiring further philosophical explanation [16].

The concept of **justified assertability** is raised as a possible alternative to truth in some discussions of meaning [17]. It proposes that understanding meaning requires examining the conditions under which assertions are justified, rather than their truth conditions [17]. This perspective aligns with pragmatist views, which emphasize the connection between meaning and practical consequences [18].

Normativist theories of meaning are subject to ongoing debate [19]. The arguments explore the relationship between meaning, correctness conditions, and rule-following [19].

For instance, **Glüer and Wikforss [20] argue** that **normativism does not necessarily exclude a naturalistic account of meaning**. They suggest that what normativists interpret as norms of meaning could be construed as **"laws of truth,"** which are neither prescriptions nor psychological laws but have a unique, potentially metaphysical necessity [20]. This perspective highlights the complex interplay between normative and metaphysical considerations in the philosophy of language.
