- Overview

As with other forms of management, the job of the project manager is to organise resources (people, time, money, materials etc.) to achieve the implicit and explicit business goals, which mainly consists of ensuring smooth operation and profitability. Project work differs from other forms of management (such as operations management, etc) in the sense that attention needs to be paid to ensure closure of the project which can often be difficult. Some reasons specific to construction for these difficulties include:

1.  Buildings are usually one off projects. This means that at the onset of the project, designs are normally not entirely flashed out. The initial design often does not fit exactly to purpose, often neglecting the specifics requirement of the site and location, furthermore, there are almost always problems with conflicting or incomplete instructions in these initial design. All of this is often discovered during construction. In other words, Construction of a large enough project is always aiming to hit a moving target.\\
2.  Building projects are inherently complex. An average house consists on average 4000 different products. Installation of these various components is almost entirely manual and requires variety skills. For each of those skillset, there is a great amount of variability in the abilities and temperament of site staff and subcontractors. The variety of activities, the interaction between people, means complexities that lead to unpredictability.\\
3.  Mistakes and omissions are almost unavoidable due to the above two condition and they end up compounding the complexity.

A project manager needs to hit this moving targets while ensuring that the project is delivered **on time**, *to* **regulation and designs**, meets **quality expectations**, **without injury** to the staff, all he while ensuring a **healthy profit margin**. The intent of this document is to explore key areas a developing project manager needs to focus on in order to carve a clear path to the end of a project. Guiding a developing project manager so he/she will have confidence to act early (planting seeds of success sort to speak) to mitigate problems before they arise.

- Leadership

\"*Being a Leader is akin to practicing Archery; when one misses the target, one seeks answer in one\'s own form\"*

- Confucius

- Principle

A leader is the person leads the way. A natural leader engenders trust in people, focuses people on the objectives, gives meaning (motivation) to the activities, recognises the special qualities each of the team members and creates the conditions for those qualities to flourish. In doing so the leader leads a team to accomplish way more than they could as a loose collective of individuals. A leader is a keeper of a vision, sells that vision, and shows people the part they play for that vision to become a reality. The best project manager is a great leader. However leadership cannot be defined in a job description and be measured in deliverables, because a poor leader can deliver the similar outcome and conform equality to a job description. The difference only shows in the level of engagement of their staff and the level of happiness in the work day.

However, a building project is not a moon landing. As a developing project manager, you are probably building something that has been done and done. All you need in terms of a vision is a clear perspective on these two questions:

\"Why are we building this building?\"

\"How are we going to build it on time, with this budget?\"

At any given stage of construction (no matter how wrong things go), if you do not have a clear perspective on these two questions, you will not be able to lead.

On a sizable project, you are expected to make hundreds, if not thousands of decisions a day. Decisions you will have to live with.
